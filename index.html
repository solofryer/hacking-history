Chapter 02

The Dawn of Hacking

During the 1960s, "hacking" wasn't about crime. At MIT, students used the term for creative ways to improve computer code and railway systems. They were the original pioneers of technology optimization.

Phone Phreaking (1970s)

Phreaks discovered they could control the telephone network using specific sound frequencies. The famous "Blue Box" allowed users to make free long-distance calls, a method used by Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak before they founded Apple.

Public Awareness (1983)

The movie 'WarGames' introduced hacking to the general public. It showed a teenager nearly starting World War III by mistake. This led to the first cyber-security laws in the United States.

The Morris Worm (1988)

Robert Tappan Morris created the first computer worm distributed via the internet. It was meant to gauge the size of the internet, but it crashed 10% of all connected systems. It was the first major cyber disaster.

Kevin Mitnick: The Legend

The "World's Most Wanted Hacker" in the 90s. He didn't just use code; he used Social Engineering to trick people into giving up passwords. He proved that humans are the weakest link in security.

Rise of Hacktivism

Anonymous emerged as a leaderless collective. Using DDoS attacks and data leaks, they target governments and corporations to protest against censorship and injustice.

Their signature Guy Fawkes mask became a symbol of digital rebellion.

The Dark Web Era

The creation of Tor allowed for anonymous browsing, leading to the rise of the Dark Web. While it provides privacy, it also birthed marketplaces like 'Silk Road' for illegal trades.

Cyber Warfare (Stuxnet)

Discovered in 2010, Stuxnet was a highly complex worm designed to physically destroy Iranian nuclear centrifuges. It proved that code can be a weapon of mass destruction in the physical world.

AI & The Future

We are now in the age of AI hacking. Artificial Intelligence can now write malware, find vulnerabilities faster than humans, and create deepfakes to deceive even the smartest users.

The battle for digital security has just begun.
